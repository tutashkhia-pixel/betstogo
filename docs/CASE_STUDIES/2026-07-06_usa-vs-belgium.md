## What BETS→GO helped clarify

- Even used loosely alongside the broadcast, the terminal's framing — "what is the highest-quality decision right now?" — changed how the match was watched: from rooting to evaluating (Lesson 1).
- Dollars-first thinking survived the couch test; probabilities did not (Lesson 2).

## What was confusing

- Without fast side-by-side outcomes (Hold / Cash Out / Hedge / Recovery / No Action) visible at a glance, the operator had to reconstruct the comparison mentally mid-match (Lesson 3).
- Data entry competed with watching: the app treated the operator like a data-entry clerk when his real value was as a field observer with eyes on the pitch (Lesson 5).

## What the operator felt under pressure

- Live matches compress decisions: by the time an offer is evaluated, it has changed.
- Emotional reactions arrive dressed up as analysis — "they look dangerous" can mean the opponent is genuinely surging, or that a goal just stung. The product should help tell those apart (Lesson 6: "Are we emotionally reacting?").

## Cash-out / hedge / recovery lessons

- Recovery temptation is strongest exactly when the engine should refuse — the rule "recovery trades must never be emotional chasing" was written from this evening (Lesson 7).
- "No Action" needed to feel like a recommendation with dignity, not an empty state (Lesson 8).
- Specific cash-out/hedge moments: **Unknown / needs operator input.**

## Product gaps exposed

1. Pulse checks captured numbers but didn't ask the *observer's* questions: Who looks better? Who looks dangerous? Is momentum shifting? Are we emotionally reacting? Has the original thesis changed? (Lesson 6)
2. The cost of inaction was never framed — doing nothing felt free (Lesson 4).
3. Nothing on screen argued *against* the operator's instinct in the moment (emotional-control gap; Lessons 6, 10).
4. Comprehension bar: if a recommendation takes longer than ~10 seconds to parse mid-match, it's too complicated — the 15-Year-Old Test was born here (Lesson 9).

## UI changes suggested

- Pulse Questions panel — the five observer prompts, permanently visible in the Live Console.
- Cost of Doing Nothing line under the Action Board.
- Devil's Advocate copy attached to every primary verdict.
- Plain-language, dollar-first labels everywhere; "No Action is valid" stated, not implied.

## What a disciplined 15-year-old trader should learn from this match

Watching a match with money on it will make your brain lie to you. The team you bet on will look better than it is; a scare will feel like a collapse. Write down what you think *before* checking the numbers, then compare. If you can't explain your next move in one sentence with real dollars in it, you don't have a move — you have a feeling. Feelings are information about you, not about the match.
